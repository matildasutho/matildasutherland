Reflection / Critique on class presentation

Like the vessel of Ursula K. Le Guin’s The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction (1986) - my presentation was not linear. It didn’t feel particularly neat, I knew there was a thread to follow in the realm of, though the exact starting point is undetermined. I was really inspired by the concrete poetry presented by Thai. The idea of using the text’s form, and to have the poems viewed “all at once” is kind of appealing. Whether I’ll explore that in this project or future projects is another question - mainly coming across the concrete poetry clarified some common themes in the articles and artworks I’d been looking at. I also need to apologise for my lack of questions to the class, it meant there wasn’t much discussion of these ideas between everybody. Though I did have a lot of fun in everybody else’s presentations... they really harnessed the etherpad which was cool I don't think we saw that so much last semester!

The first article I looked at is Diligence, Patience and Humility, written by Larry Wall (1999). Wall discusses the nature of human pattern-making, symbolism-seeking and oversimplifying nature in relation to writing, and more specifically coding and the Perl language. What really caught my attention is the diagrams along the way. Wall cleverly uses the simple starting point of a line drawing of a circle within a rectangle, adding and expanding - into, pearl onions, yinyang, a smiley, Mars/Venus, ending on the door to the hobbit Bilbo Baggins’ house. Walls storytelling draws you in with the writing, the symbols unfolding throughout add a layer of meaning to the story itself.

The next article I looked at is Body Building: the code performance of Joana Chicau (Daniel Temkin, 2019). Temkin points to Chicua’s use of the circle in her live coding performances, to break the linear, rectangular, 90 degree’d tools for organising web pages. Chicau works with errors, drawing on the circle’s lack of beginning and end, the performance pieces evolve at random. Like the Carrier Bag Theory, Chicau’s performance’s are a non-linear, and slightly messy, with soft angles as Temkin notes, Chicau “…is surfing just above a collapse of errors”.

I think this is why I enjoyed the performance of The Cowgirl Manifesto, because there were errors and they were an integral part of it. I didn’t feel a pressure to “get it right”, because there was no “right” way to do it. In fact the whole preparation for the performance was really about having the tools at my disposal that allowed for improvisation. The vessel contained the separate pieces of the poem, different code manipulations, as standalone features - allowing me to paste the pieces almost randomly and still have an effect.

Part of me would like to attempt SuperCollider, a text-based live coded music program, to create a short performance of the song I’ve created titled “Dirtstyle”. But it could be a more valuable exercise to attempt to translate the poem into a digital concrete poem of sorts.

A couple of works I’m attracted to are Date-Night by Sophie Narrett (2016), and Capricorn LG by Brian Khek (2013). In Date Night, Narrett embroiders people in surreal meshes of indoor/outdoor scenes, naked, with VR headsets on. Khek critiques the upgrade culture of white goods through videos of kitchen appliances at various angles with captions beneath for each shot. Through their materiality and subject matter, each of these works explore upgrade culture. Narrett’s depictions of a VR date-night suggest disparity between the promises of a technological future, and the realities of life dominated by tech and digital infrastructure - the consequences this has for human interaction, and human’s broader ecological connection. Khek’s Capricorn LG (2013), is shot on an iPhone, in portrait mode, with captions across the bottom making comments such as: "It worked so well I had to buy another”. These portrait style videos with witty text overlaid are a timely prediction of the influx of Instagram ‘Reels’ and TikTok culture to come.

Rather than thinking of Dirtstyle as purely a net.art movement, I’d like to focus on its’ exploration of upgrade culture - and not being confined to web-based projects. The IRL world and tech is only becoming more integrated, with digital life no longer confined to the browser. Scott Benesiinaabandan writes in Gwiizens, the Old Lady and the Octopus Bag Device (2020), the relation of adizkookan (sacred stories) and the woven octopus bag device, to tech. In telling the story as he remembers (as he can not find the original source) he repurposes it and meshes it with similar stories he ahs come across, and intertwines his own practice in AI. In this repurposing he highlights the relevance of these old stories to digital cultures. Like Wall (1999), he uses the imagery and metaphor to discuss human nature in the broader scheme of technologies and ecologies.

And so “…there are seeds to be gathered, and room in the bag of stars” (Le Guin, 1986).
